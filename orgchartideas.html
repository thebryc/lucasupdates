Peer Leads
COS (HR/Mgmt items): Chasity, Jakia, and Rhonda bring HR and management matters to Josh. Curriculum matters go to Elin. This reporting structure may evolve over time as the organization grows.
Relationships: Chasity ↔ Aareena share ownership of the 10th–11th grade Fellow experience. Jakia ↔ Rhonda have significant overlap serving seniors through counseling and advising. These are lateral, collaborative relationships — not reporting lines.
Why this works: Research on shared and peer leadership consistently shows that lateral structures outperform traditional top-down models in environments requiring cross-functional coordination. Studies on peer leadership behaviors find that when colleagues lead horizontally — sharing accountability, providing mutual feedback, and surfacing problems early — teams produce more sustainable outcomes than those dependent on vertical escalation alone. Research on shared leadership in top management teams shows that distributing leadership across peers, rather than concentrating it in a single authority, improves both decision quality and team cohesion — particularly in organizations where different domains (curriculum, HR, programs) require different kinds of expertise.

Jay Galbraith's work on matrix organizations provides the structural framing: this model works when the two "matrix managers" (Josh and Elin) are in constant alignment. The peer check-ins between director pairs are the horizontal glue — they resolve roughly 80% of overlaps themselves, escalating only the remaining 20% (management issues to Josh, curriculum issues to Elin). This also enables dynamic resource allocation: if the advising team is overwhelmed with college application deadlines, the learning team — through their peer check-in — can adjust curriculum timing to support the same cohort of seniors, with Elin's blessing, without requiring a full leadership meeting to make it happen.

Alumni Context
BRYC currently has 920 alumni, with ~134 graduating seniors joining in May. The team prioritizes Fellows within their first two years post-graduation — the most vulnerable window for summer melt and second-fall drop-off. That cohort currently totals 253 students. The broader alumni base spans all years, though most active engagement is concentrated in the most recent graduating classes.
What Alumni Actually Want
Alumni want to be able to access BRYC and get support when they need it. Formal programs like SHRM or Exxon are valued additions, but they are not what alumni are clamoring for. The core ask is simpler: be reachable, be responsive, and be real. Direct aid (basic needs, emergency support) and mentoring connections are the two areas of growing demand.
Model Direction
Leaning toward a peer-to-peer, small group hub model — committed alumni leaders facilitate monthly gatherings (career + community focus) with 8–10 regular attendees, compensated via stipend. This removes the burden from managers to create and run everything, freeing capacity for high-need individual cases. See Braven as a comparable model worth studying.
Additional Priorities
Institutional relationships: Someone should own deep, ongoing connections with colleges (admissions, registrars, student affairs) and corporations (career connections, opportunities) — not transactional, but sustained partnerships.

Persistence 101: The Persistence team facilitates this postsecondary next-steps program in spring for Senior Fellows, taking the place of Program B in their weekly schedule. This is a key bridge between the learning team and Persistence.

Principle: BRYC doesn't need to do too much — but it shouldn't be purely reactive. The goal is a lightweight, scalable model that meets alumni where they are and keeps the door open.
Level 1

Manager

$35K–$60K
Scope
Executes at a high level within a defined purview. Willing and able to lead on opportunities to deepen that purview. Contributes to strategic planning efforts.
Self-Direction
Mostly self-directed within their lane, with frequent (weekly or more) coaching and high-touch management. Proactively raises issues — sometimes with solutions, sometimes not.
Planning Horizon
Weekly. Drives planning of their own duties with guidance from team meetings and supervisor check-ins.
Culture
High commitment to professionalism and maturity. Actively develops through multiple coaching conversations per year around BRYC's core values.
Winning Habits
Executes the weekly plan and adjusts as needed. Takes detailed notes. Proactively keeps director updated. Identifies problems and brings solutions.
Level 2

Director

$60K–$80K
Scope
Content command and leadership credibility to manage one or more teams. Responsible for crafting a strategic vision, building necessary infrastructure, and driving execution.
Self-Direction
Completely self-directed with relatively infrequent, high-level coaching. Drives team-wide execution. Brings potential answers to almost all questions to their supervisor.
Planning Horizon
Semesterly. Sets tone, communicates vision, delegates to managers, and provides appropriately detailed guidance and coaching to direct reports.
Culture
Consummate professional and sterling BRYC representative — internally and externally. Consistently exemplifies BRYC's core values at the highest level.
Winning Habits
Leads meetings; sends WWW recaps. Holds direct reports accountable to communication standards. Proactively harvests resources and builds systems for their team.
Level 3

Chief

$80K+
Scope
Organizational-level accountability. Sets and enforces the vision across multiple teams and functions. Directly responsible for a domain of BRYC's mission — and for the people executing it.
Self-Direction
Fully self-directed. Coaches and thought-partners with directors so they and their teams are positioned to win. Reserves direct execution for areas of personal ownership.
Planning Horizon
Annual. Sets and enforces OKRAs across the organization. Ensures all involved teammates are clear on what winning looks like — and holds that standard.
Culture
Upholds and enforces BRYC's cultural standard across the organization. Regularly engages with Fellows, Guardians, and Mentors to hold the customer service standard personally.
Winning Habits
Enforces post-effort follow-up across the org. Proactively harvests resources and builds org-wide systems. Holds directors accountable to communication and planning standards.
